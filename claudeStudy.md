# Tuning a Volume-Breakout Fade on Hyperliquid Perps: A Literature-Grounded Parameter Guide

## TL;DR
- Your core thesis (fade abnormal-volume breakouts as a liquidity-provision/overreaction reversal) is well-supported by both TradFi (Nagel 2012; de Groot–Huij–Zhou 2011; Jegadeesh 1990/Lehmann 1990) and crypto (Liu–Tsyvinski–Wu; Dobrynskaya) literature — but your in-sample Sharpe ~3.8 is almost certainly inflated by lookahead in the regime quantiles, signal time-clustering, and multiple testing. Deflate it before trusting it.
- The highest-value, most literature-supported changes to test: (a) replace the raw 5x-median volume gate with a rolling volume z-score, (b) decouple your single 24h lookback into separate range/vol/median windows and set the holding period from an estimated Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) half-life, and (c) upgrade the funding filter from a mere sign-match to a funding-EXTREME gate — the strongest academic evidence (BIS "Crypto Carry") shows extreme funding predicts crashes because it reflects crowded trend-chasers.
- Keep running without a hard price stop (the "negative skew is the premium" view has real empirical support), but add tail controls that don't truncate the edge: volatility-scaled sizing, a correlation/concurrency cap, and time-based exits calibrated to half-life — plus a wide catastrophe-only stop tested purely as cheap insurance against liquidation cascades.

## Key Findings

**1. The strategy sits on solid theoretical ground.** Short-term reversal returns are widely interpreted as compensation for providing liquidity to urgent, price-pressuring order flow (Nagel 2012, *Review of Financial Studies*; Jegadeesh 1990; Lehmann 1990). Your "volume-spike-at-breakout" is a clean proxy for exactly the exhaustion/overreaction event that reverses. In crypto specifically, Liu, Tsyvinski & Wu document a strong daily reversal driven by illiquidity that concentrates in less-liquid coins — directly matching your finding that edge concentrates in MID-liquidity names, while the largest/most-tradeable coins actually show daily *momentum*.

**2. Your Sharpe is likely inflated — quantify the haircut.** You already flag full-sample quantile thresholds (lookahead), time-clustering, and in-sample-only results. The Bailey–López de Prado Deflated Sharpe Ratio (2014) exists precisely for this: it corrects the Sharpe for selection bias under multiple testing plus non-normal (negatively skewed, fat-tailed) returns. Harvey, Liu & Zhu (2016, *RFS* 29(1):5–68) — who document at least 316 tested factors — conclude "a new factor needs to clear a much higher hurdle, with a t-statistic greater than 3.0," not the conventional 2.0. Expect a meaningful haircut once you account for how many configurations you've tried.

**3. Volatility conditioning is a genuine, exploitable lever.** Nagel (2012, "Evaporating Liquidity") finds that reversal "expected returns and conditional Sharpe Ratios increase enormously along with the VIX during times of financial market turmoil, such as the financial crisis 2007-09." Liquidity provision pays most when volatility is high and liquidity supply withdraws. This validates your high-vol regime filter and suggests you can push the cutoff harder.

**4. Funding is subtle — and crypto-specific.** The strongest academic evidence, Schmeling, Schrimpf & Todorov, "Crypto Carry" (BIS Working Paper 1087, April 2023; now published in *Management Science*, 2026, doi:10.1287/mnsc.2024.05069), finds high crypto carry/funding predicts future price crashes because it reflects crowded, trend-chasing retail longs and limited arbitrage capital. Concretely, they estimate that "an increase in the standardized carry by 10% predicts an increase in liquidations of short futures positions by 22% of total open interest over the next month," with carry sometimes exceeding 40–60% p.a. This supports fading the crowded side — but implies the signal is strongest at funding EXTREMES, not merely when funding sign matches breakout direction.

## Details

### A) Tuning your existing levers

**Volume-spike multiple (currently 5x median).**
The abnormal-trading-volume (ATV) reversal literature (e.g., Ülkü et al., *Journal of Forecasting* 2019) generally *normalizes* volume rather than using a raw multiple. The most robust practice is a **volume z-score**: (current − rolling mean)/rolling std, ideally on **log-volume** to tame crypto's heavy right tail. Recommendation: keep 5x median as one arm but add a **volume z-score gate of +2 to +3** (roughly analogous to 5x), and sweep the multiple across **{3x, 4x, 5x, 7x, 10x}** on the median. Median is the more robust baseline (crypto volume is extremely fat-tailed); log-volume z-score is the most defensible normalization and makes the gate comparable across coins and regimes. Hypothesis: z-score normalization tightens the signal and reduces coin-to-coin/regime-to-regime instability.

**Lookback window (currently 24h for range, vol-median, and realized vol).**
The literature does NOT support a single coupled window. Short-term reversal is estimated over horizons from a month (equities: Jegadeesh 1990; de Groot weekly) down to intraday (crypto: reversal documented on 5-min/hourly Bitcoin data). Recommendation: **decouple** into (i) breakout range lookback, (ii) volume-median lookback, (iii) realized-vol lookback. Test breakout at **{12h, 24h, 48h}**, volume-median at **{12h, 24h}**, and set the realized-vol window shorter/more responsive (Moreira–Muir use ~22-day daily variance in equities, but intraday you want faster reaction). Most importantly, set your HOLDING window from the OU half-life (below), not from the entry lookback.

**Realized-vol regime filter (currently 60th pct, keep top 40%).**
Nagel (2012) is the anchor cite: reversal pays most when volatility is high, validating your filter and suggesting you test a **more aggressive cutoff** — sweep **{50th, 60th, 70th, 80th}** percentile. Two caveats: (1) in crypto, *low* realized vol can mask leverage buildup that precedes cascades (Amberdata's Oct-2025 cascade analysis found the lowest-vol regime immediately preceded the crash), so extreme realized vol may occasionally signal continuation risk rather than reversion; (2) the CRITICAL lookahead fix — compute the percentile on a **trailing/expanding window**, never full-sample. This single change may cut your Sharpe materially but makes it real.

**Funding-rate / carry alignment (currently sign-match).**
This is your subtlest lever. "Crypto Carry" (Schmeling–Schrimpf–Todorov) shows high carry is driven by trend-chasing retail longs and predicts crashes — so fading a crowded long (up-break + high positive funding → short) is well-motivated, and their liquidation result quantifies the crowding-unwind channel. But the effect concentrates at EXTREMES. Recommendation: replace the binary sign-match with a **funding-extremity gate** — require funding to be in the top/bottom decile or above a z-score threshold. Practitioner convention (CoinGlass, Sharpe AI, Altrady, checkonchain) uses a **30-day funding z-score**, with extreme thresholds cited around **+0.05% to +0.1% per 8h (~55–110% annualized)**, and heuristics like ">100% APR = top signal," "persistent >+30% APR for 3+ days often precedes 5–15% pullbacks within 24–72h." Test: (a) current sign-match, (b) sign-match AND funding z-score > 1.5, (c) sign-match AND funding in top/bottom decile. Hypothesis: conditioning on funding extremity raises per-trade edge but cuts trade count.

Important honesty caveats: there is NO rigorous published cross-sectional coin-level study sorting coins by funding rate and reporting forward-return spreads (the established crypto cross-sectional factors are size, 2-week momentum, value, and trend — not funding), so treat the specific magnitude thresholds above as practitioner heuristics, not validated numbers. And every serious source stresses funding can stay extreme through strong trends — fading it too early loses money. This is exactly why your volume-exhaustion + breakout co-trigger matters: it times the unwind rather than fading funding blindly.

**Exit timing (currently reclaim OR 8h backstop, no stop).**
Two literatures apply. (1) **OU half-life:** estimate mean-reversion speed θ by fitting an OU/AR(1) process to the post-signal price path (discrete form: regress ΔP on lagged P, slope λ; half-life = −ln(2)/λ), then set the time backstop to roughly **1–2× half-life** (Ernie Chan's standard method; arbitragelab / Hudson & Thames implementations). If empirical half-life on your signals is 2–4 hours, your 8h backstop may be holding through regime shifts; if it's 6–8h, 8h is about right. Sweep backstops at **{half-life, 1.5×, 2×, fixed 4h, 6h, 8h, 12h}**. Hudson & Thames caution the OU estimate is unreliable with fewer than ~20 mean-crossings in-window — so use it as a filter too. (2) **Stop-loss debate:** research genuinely supports running WITHOUT a hard stop for negatively-skewed MR. Price Action Lab's B2S2 test showed a 1% stop turned skew from −2.51 to +0.60 but LOWERED annualized return and RAISED max drawdown; a multi-pair statarb study (arXiv 2406.10695) found stops had only "moderate negative impact." Your instinct is defensible. BUT Leung & Li (2015, "Optimal Mean Reversion Trading with Transaction Costs and Stop-Loss Exit") show an optimal stop CAN coexist with an OU entry band — so test a very wide **catastrophe-only stop** (e.g., 3–5× ATR, or a fixed % well beyond normal excursion) purely to cap liquidation-cascade tail risk, not to cut normal losers. Also test a modest **profit target** (exit at reclaim vs. holding to mid-range).

**Bar interval (currently 5m and 15m).**
Crypto intraday reversal is documented on 5-min and hourly Bitcoin data, and the reversal effect is "faster" in crypto than equities (Dobrynskaya: cross-sectional momentum flips to reversal after ~1 month vs. longer in equities; intraday studies find reversal at the hour scale). Higher frequency = more signals but more microstructure noise and more sensitivity to your 1.5bps maker fee. Recommendation: keep 5m and 15m, and add a **1m** arm and a **30m/1h** arm to map the decay curve. Expect edge-per-trade to rise with bar size while trade count falls; document which arm dominates net-of-fees.

**Position sizing & concurrency (currently $100 flat, 40-position cap).**
Two robust findings. (1) **Volatility-scaling:** Moreira–Muir (2017) and Harvey et al. ("The Impact of Volatility Targeting," Man Group) show inverse-variance scaling improves risk-adjusted returns for risky/long-short strategies — replace flat $100 with **notional ∝ 1/realized-vol** (constant risk per trade). Caveat: Cederburg et al. (2020) find the real-time benefit is weaker than in-sample and comes largely from combining managed + unmanaged exposure, so implement as a combination and validate OOS. (2) **Kelly under negative skew:** full Kelly assuming normality badly OVERBETS negatively-skewed strategies (the structural cause of the Feb-2018 short-vol blowups). Use **quarter- to half-Kelly at most**. Crucially, correlated concurrent positions multiply effective bet size — your 40-position cap on *time-clustered* signals is likely far more concentrated than 40 independent bets. Add a **correlation/crowding cap**: limit simultaneous same-direction positions and scale down aggregate exposure when many signals fire together.

**Universe / liquidity (currently HIGH/MID tiers; edge in MID).**
This is one of your best-supported findings. de Groot–Huij–Zhou (2011) show reversal profits (30–50 bps/week net of costs) survive mainly in liquid names — small-caps destroy the edge via transaction costs. Nagel and the crypto liquidity-provision literature (Fieberg et al.) show reversal/liquidity-provision returns concentrate in smaller, more volatile, less-liquid pairs where adverse-selection fear is higher. Liu–Tsyvinski–Wu: the largest, most tradeable coins show daily MOMENTUM, not reversal. So your MID-tier concentration is exactly what theory predicts — illiquid enough to earn the liquidity-provision premium, liquid enough that your maker fills and 1.5bps cost don't eat it. Recommendation: formalize this — test finer liquidity buckets (quintiles by 24h notional), confirm edge is monotone across the mid-buckets, and explicitly exclude the very-top-liquidity bucket if it shows momentum.

### B) Alternative signal constructions

**Z-score / Bollinger entries.** Classic MR: enter when price z-score vs a rolling MA exceeds ±2, exit at 0 (the mean). Standard params: 20-period MA, 2 std-dev bands; quant convention enters at |z|>2, exits at z→0. Bollinger is essentially the continuous version of your discrete range-pierce. Test as an ALTERNATIVE trigger (with your volume + funding gates layered on): does fading a +2σ excursion beat fading a range-breakout? Lowering the band threshold increases trades, profits, and Sharpe but also volatility/drawdown (ETF pairs evidence).

**Ornstein–Uhlenbeck half-life.** Fit dX = θ(μ−X)dt + σdW. Use it two ways: (1) as a FILTER — only trade signals whose post-signal series has a short, stable half-life; (2) to SET the holding backstop (1–2× half-life). This is the single most principled way to set exit timing and directly addresses your "8h backstop — is it right?" question.

**RSI / oscillator reversal.** Connors-style RSI(2) with oversold <10 / overbought >90 is the canonical short-term MR oscillator; RSI(14) with 30/70 is the slower standard. Shorter periods (7–9) generate more intraday signals. Test RSI(2) or RSI(3) extremes as an additional confirmation gate or an alternative trigger.

**Cross-sectional vs. time-series MR.** Your strategy is time-series (per-asset). The crypto literature strongly documents CROSS-SECTIONAL short-term reversal (Liu–Tsyvinski–Wu: rank coins by last-day return, long losers / short winners; Dobrynskaya: "past loser" coins drive reversal). Cross-sectional is often more robust because it is market-neutral and diversifies idiosyncratic noise — important in crypto where BTC beta dominates. Recommendation: build a cross-sectional overlay — at each bar, rank your universe by recent return and fade the extremes, market-neutral — and test whether it beats (or de-risks) your per-asset fade.

**Statistical arbitrage / pairs (Avellaneda–Lee).** The canonical stat-arb models PCA/factor residuals as an OU process and trades the s-score. Avellaneda & Lee (2010, *Quantitative Finance* 10(7):761–782) open at |s|>1.25 and close at 0.5 (long) / 0.75 (short); their PCA strategy delivered, after transaction costs, "an average annual Sharpe ratio of 1.44 over the period 1997 to 2007," degrading to "only 0.9" during 2003–2007 (ETF-based = 1.1; ETF + volume/"trading time" = 1.51 in 2003–2007). Crypto cointegration works (Leung–Nguyen 2019 calibrate OU half-life for asset selection; BTC–ETH pairs reported with Sharpe up to ~2.45). This is complementary — a market-neutral perp sleeve that would diversify your directional fade book.

**Order-flow / VPIN.** VPIN (Easley–López de Prado–O'Hara) measures order-flow toxicity on a volume clock. Crypto research (Kitvanitphasu et al., 2025, on Bitcoin) finds "VPIN significantly predicts future price jumps," with positive serial correlation in VPIN and jump size — i.e., toxic flow tends to *continue*. On Hyperliquid, VPIN, CVD, and order-flow imbalance are computable from the trade tape (Buildix computes them live; a VPIN spike >0.7 flags one-sided directional pressure). Recommendation: test VPIN / order-flow-imbalance as a CONFIRMATION filter on your volume spike — a highly TOXIC, one-sided spike (informed flow) is more likely to *continue* (don't fade), whereas a *balanced* or liquidation-driven spike is more likely exhaustion (fade). This directly sharpens the exhaustion-vs-continuation discrimination that is the crux of your thesis.

### C) Synthesis: prioritized experiments to backtest

**Tier 1 — fix validity first (do before trusting any result):**
1. Recompute ALL regime quantiles (vol percentile, funding thresholds, liquidity terciles) on trailing/expanding windows only. Eliminates the lookahead you flagged. Hypothesized effect: Sharpe drops but becomes real. Literature: López de Prado (purged CV).
2. Implement walk-forward / purged K-fold CV with an embargo around each fold to handle time-clustering; report the Deflated Sharpe Ratio accounting for the number of configurations tried. Literature: Bailey–López de Prado 2014; Harvey–Liu–Zhu 2016 (t ≥ 3.0).
3. Block-bootstrap the trade series (blocks preserve clustering) for honest confidence intervals on +25bps/trade and 56% win rate.

**Tier 2 — highest-expected-value parameter changes:**
4. Volume gate: log-volume z-score > {2, 2.5, 3} vs. multiple {3,4,5,7,10}×median. Basis: ATV/Ülkü normalization literature.
5. Decouple lookbacks; grid breakout {12,24,48}h × short vol-window × median {12,24}h. Basis: intraday-reversal horizon evidence.
6. OU half-life: estimate per-signal, set backstop {1×,1.5×,2× half-life}; also filter to short-half-life signals. Basis: Chan / arbitragelab / Leung–Nguyen.
7. Funding: sign-match vs. sign-match + extremity (z>1.5 or top/bottom decile). Basis: Schmeling–Schrimpf–Todorov "Crypto Carry."
8. Vol-scaled sizing (notional ∝ 1/realized-vol) at quarter-to-half-Kelly + same-direction concurrency/correlation cap. Basis: Moreira–Muir; Thorp fractional Kelly; Cederburg caveat.

**Tier 3 — new signals / robustness:**
9. Cross-sectional reversal overlay (rank-and-fade, market-neutral). Basis: Liu–Tsyvinski–Wu; Dobrynskaya.
10. VPIN / order-flow-imbalance confirmation (fade balanced spikes, skip toxic one-sided ones). Basis: Kitvanitphasu et al. 2025.
11. Wide catastrophe-only stop (3–5× ATR) tested purely for cascade/tail protection. Basis: Leung–Li 2015 vs. Price Action Lab.
12. Bollinger/z-score and RSI(2) alternative triggers; frequency sweep {1m,5m,15m,30m,1h}.

### Avoiding overfitting / lookahead
- **Walk-forward & purged CV with embargo** (López de Prado) — mandatory given time-clustering.
- **Deflated Sharpe Ratio** (Bailey–López de Prado 2014) — deflate by the number of trials; you've tested many configs.
- **Harvey–Liu–Zhu haircut** — require t-stat ≥ ~3.0, not 2.0, for any new lever to count as real.
- **White's Reality Check / Hansen's SPA** — test whether the best config beats a benchmark after accounting for the full search.
- **Trailing quantiles everywhere** — never full-sample percentiles.
- **Out-of-sample holdout** — reserve the most recent data untouched until the very end.

### Where crypto microstructure means TradFi findings may NOT transfer
- **Funding rate** has no TradFi analog; the crash-predictive carry dynamic (BIS "Crypto Carry") is crypto-specific, and its sign/magnitude behavior differs from equity short-interest or borrow-fee signals.
- **Liquidation cascades** create fat left tails and reflexive *continuation* that can overwhelm mean reversion precisely during your high-vol/high-volume triggers — the single biggest threat to a no-stop book, and the reason to test a catastrophe stop. (VPIN evidence that toxic flow predicts *continuing* jumps reinforces this.)
- **24/7 trading** — no overnight gaps/session resets; equity intraday-reversal findings (open-to-close, end-of-day reversal à la Baltussen–Da–Soebhag) don't map cleanly.
- **Perp basis / thin books** — MID-liquidity Hyperliquid names can have thin books where your own maker orders and slippage matter; de Groot's "liquid names only" logic still applies but the liquidity frontier is different from equities.
- **Beta to BTC** dominates the crypto cross-section; a per-asset time-series fade carries hidden market beta that a cross-sectional/market-neutral construction would remove.

## Recommendations
1. **Immediately:** re-run with trailing-only quantiles and compute a Deflated Sharpe. Do not scale capital until OOS/deflated numbers hold. Proceed threshold: deflated Sharpe still comfortably positive (target >1.5) and t-stat ≥ 3 after the Harvey–Liu haircut. If it collapses, the edge was mostly lookahead.
2. **Next:** run the Tier-2 parameter grids under walk-forward. Adopt any change only if it improves OOS deflated Sharpe — never in-sample Sharpe.
3. **Then:** add vol-scaled sizing + a correlation/concurrency cap (the highest-value risk change given time-clustered signals), and set exits from the estimated OU half-life rather than a fixed 8h.
4. **Explore:** the cross-sectional reversal overlay and VPIN confirmation as the two most promising new signals; add a small market-neutral stat-arb sleeve for diversification.
5. **Keep** the no-hard-stop stance but add a catastrophe-only stop (3–5× ATR) as cheap cascade insurance; benchmark it explicitly — if it lowers deflated Sharpe and raises drawdown (as Price Action Lab found for tight stops), drop it. The benchmark that would change this decision: a stop that reduces tail drawdown without lowering deflated Sharpe is worth keeping.

## Caveats
- Reported +25bps/trade, 56% win, Sharpe 3.8 are in-sample with acknowledged lookahead; treat as an upper bound, not an expectation.
- Many funding-rate threshold numbers are practitioner heuristics (CoinGlass, Sharpe AI, Altrady), not peer-reviewed; validate them on your own data. There is no rigorous coin-level funding-sorted cross-sectional study to lean on.
- Volatility-targeting's real-time benefit is contested (Cederburg et al. 2020) — implement as a combination and confirm OOS.
- Crypto reversal/carry edges are time-varying and have compressed recently: Borri, Liu, Tsyvinski & Wu ("Cryptocurrency as an Investable Asset Class: Coming of Age," arXiv:2510.14435) report the crypto-carry strategy's "annualized Sharpe ratio is 6.45" full-sample (2020–2025) but that "beginning in 2024, the Sharpe ratio falls to 4.06, and it turns negative in 2025." An edge that worked over your 8-month sample may decay — re-estimate on rolling windows.
- Nagel's high-vol result is from equity crises; in crypto, the high-vol regime is also where liquidation-cascade continuation risk is greatest, so the volatility filter is a double-edged sword — pair it with the order-flow/toxicity confirmation.
